## The community you are building for

Tatweer is rooted in Al Qua'a, a community in the Al Ain region of the UAE. The challenges below come from real needs here, but a strong solution can serve communities like it across the country and beyond.

- Al Qua'a sits right on the Tropic of Cancer, one of the few inhabited places that does.
- A large share of families here run camel farms, which for many is a main source of income.
- It is known globally as one of the best places for stargazing, thanks to its very low light pollution.

Keep this context in mind. The best projects feel built for this place and its people, not generic.

## The challenge track

The track is **digital solutions for rural communities**. Pick **one** of the five challenges below and build for it. All five are judged on the same criteria and carry equal weight. We offer a range so you can choose where your skills show best. Low code solutions and deeply technical ones are judged equally. What matters is the value you create and how well you prove it.

## The five challenges

### Challenge 1 — Taking the first entrepreneurial step
- **The problem.** Many people here have a viable idea or a real skill but never take the first step toward turning it into a business. The barrier is rarely ambition, it is not knowing what the first move is, what is required, or where to begin.
- **Who it is for.** A first time founder in the community who has an idea or a skill but has never started a business.
- **A good solution.** Lowers that barrier and helps a first time founder move from an idea to a concrete first action.
- **For example.** A guided tool that turns an idea into a clear action plan, a simple way to understand the steps, costs, or licenses a small local business needs, or something that connects a new founder to the first resource, customer, or person they need.

### Challenge 2 — Reaching people quickly across a dispersed community
- **The problem.** The community is spread across a wide area, and when someone needs urgent help, the time it takes for the right response to reach them matters. Distance and dispersion work against speed.
- **Who it is for.** A resident who needs urgent help, and the people or services trying to reach them in time.
- **A good solution.** Shortens the gap between a need arising and the right response reaching the person, in a setting where distance slows things down.
- **For example.** A way to share a precise location and reach the nearest available help fast, a system that coordinates or routes responders more efficiently across a wide area, or a tool that helps a community respond in the first minutes before formal help arrives.

### Challenge 3 — The data gap for local entrepreneurs
- **The problem.** Entrepreneurs here often make decisions in the dark. There is little local data on customers, demand, or what the area actually needs, so people guess.
- **Who it is for.** A local entrepreneur or small business owner deciding what to build, what to sell, or where to focus.
- **A good solution.** Helps a local entrepreneur gather, access, or make sense of data about their own community so they can decide with evidence instead of guesswork.
- **For example.** A simple way to collect and visualize local demand, a tool that turns scattered local information into usable insight, or a lightweight market research helper for someone with no data team.

### Challenge 4 — Connecting residents to services, opportunities and events
- **The problem.** People are not always aware of the local services, opportunities, and events available to them, and there is no simple way to find them or stay informed.
- **Who it is for.** A resident who would benefit from a service, opportunity, or event but does not know it exists.
- **A good solution.** Makes it easier for residents to discover, reach, and stay informed about what is available in their community.
- **For example.** A single place to find local services and events, a way to match residents to opportunities relevant to them, or a simple discovery and notification tool for what is happening nearby.

### Challenge 5 — Free choice, solve a real problem in your community
- **The problem.** This is the open lane. You identify a real problem facing your own community and build a solution for it. The problem must be genuine and rooted in a real community. The solution can be anything.
- **Who it is for.** Whoever your chosen problem affects. Define them clearly.
- **A good solution.** Names a real, local problem and the people it affects, then builds something that meaningfully addresses it.
- **For example.** Anything the other four challenges do not cover, as long as the problem is real. Some of the most original work comes from here, including ideas drawn from the community's own life and livelihoods.

## Who can take part

- Open to everyone. There is no minimum and no nationality requirement.
- You can take part on your own or as a team of up to five members.
- Form or join your team through the platform after registering.

## What you build and submit

- Every team submits **one public GitHub repository**, whatever the project type. An app, a website, a hardware build, a drone, a robot, or a research study all go in the repo: code, files, documents, and a demo video.
- AI and all other tools are permitted, with no restrictions.
- The repository must be **public** so it can be reviewed.
- Only commits made before the **Saturday 8:00 PM GST** deadline are counted.

## What your README must cover

Your repository README is how you are understood and scored. It must cover all of the following, and these map directly to the judging criteria:

- The challenge you chose, and the specific problem within it.
- The target demographic and the situation they face.
- Your solution, and the impact it makes with testable claims.
- Feasibility and deployment, and scalability beyond the event.
- How to run or verify it, the tools you used, and any evidence or validation.

## How to submit

You submit through a form that asks for the link to your project's GitHub repository. The form link will be posted here and sent to all participants. Keep your repository public and ready to share, and submit before the deadline. Late submissions are not counted.

## How you are judged

There are two rounds.

- **Round one, filtration.** Saturday from 10:00 PM, scored entirely from your GitHub repository on criteria 1 to 7. The totals rank all teams and the top 10 advance.
- **Round two, the final.** Sunday, the top 10 present live. The same criteria are re scored on what is shown, and criterion 8 is added.

Each judge scores every criterion using the bands below. Scores are summed per judge, then averaged across judges to give your total. Teams are ranked by total.

- **Filtration total: 65 points**
- **Final total: 75 points**
- Tiebreak order is Impact first, then Feasibility.
- A criterion with no evidence in the repo is scored in its lowest band, not skipped. So document everything.

### 1 — Impact and value to the community (10 pts)
The size and realness of the benefit to the target group.

| Band | Description |
|---|---|
| 0 to 2 | No clear beneficiary or benefit. Impact asserted but not tied to a real need. |
| 3 to 5 | Some benefit, but narrow, marginal, or weakly tied to a real need. |
| 6 to 8 | Clear, meaningful benefit to a defined group, addressing a genuine need. |
| 9 to 10 | Substantial benefit to many, addressing a pressing need, clearly articulated. |

### 2 — Relevance to the challenge (10 pts)
How directly it addresses the track and a real problem within it.

| Band | Description |
|---|---|
| 0 to 2 | Off topic or only loosely connected to the challenge. |
| 3 to 5 | Related, but the link to the challenge is partial or generic. |
| 6 to 8 | Clearly addresses the challenge and a specific problem within it. |
| 9 to 10 | Squarely on the challenge, targeting a well chosen, high value problem. |

### 3 — Feasibility of implementation (10 pts)
Whether it can realistically be deployed, considering cost, resources, and maintenance.

| Band | Description |
|---|---|
| 0 to 2 | Not implementable in any realistic setting, or ignores cost and resources. |
| 3 to 5 | Implementable only with major unaddressed obstacles or unrealistic assumptions. |
| 6 to 8 | Implementable with reasonable resources, main constraints acknowledged. |
| 9 to 10 | Clearly deployable in a rural setting, with cost, resources, and maintenance thought through. |

### 4 — Readiness of the solution (10 pts)
How complete and functional the solution is now.

| Band | Description |
|---|---|
| 0 to 2 | Idea only, nothing built. |
| 3 to 5 | Early prototype or partial mockup, not demonstrable end to end. |
| 6 to 8 | Working build that demonstrates the core function. |
| 9 to 10 | Complete, working solution demonstrated end to end. |

### 5 — Scalability after the hackathon (10 pts)
Whether it can grow beyond the event and replicate to other communities.

| Band | Description |
|---|---|
| 0 to 2 | One off, cannot grow or repeat. |
| 3 to 5 | Could grow, but with serious technical or operational limits. |
| 6 to 8 | Scales to more users or sites with manageable effort. |
| 9 to 10 | Designed to scale and replicate, with a clear path to growth. |

### 6 — Falsifiability and evidence (10 pts)
Whether the team makes specific, testable claims backed by evidence, not vague hype.

| Band | Description |
|---|---|
| 0 to 2 | Vague, unfalsifiable claims, no evidence. |
| 3 to 5 | Some claims are specific, but largely unsupported. |
| 6 to 8 | Clear, testable claims with some supporting evidence or testing. |
| 9 to 10 | Specific, testable claims backed by data, testing, or community validation. |

### 7 — Repo documentation and completeness (5 pts)
Whether the repo contains everything needed to understand and verify the work.

| Band | Description |
|---|---|
| 0 to 1 | Sparse or missing README, hard to understand. |
| 2 to 3 | README present but incomplete, gaps in what is needed to verify. |
| 4 to 5 | Complete README and repo, everything present to understand, run, or verify, including demo material. |

### 8 — Presentation and live demo (10 pts, Sunday final only)
Clarity of the live demonstration and how well the team answers questions.

| Band | Description |
|---|---|
| 0 to 2 | Unclear, no working demonstration, cannot answer questions. |
| 3 to 5 | Basic presentation, partial demo, weak on questions. |
| 6 to 8 | Clear presentation, working demo, handles most questions well. |
| 9 to 10 | Compelling presentation, strong working demo, credible under questioning. |
